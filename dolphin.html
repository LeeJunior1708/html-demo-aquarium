Dolphin

Dolphin - very friendly animals

Dolphins are mammals and are closely related to Elephants. Dolphins, like whales, are descendants of terrestrial mammals, and may all belong to the phylum Artiodactyl. The ancestors of today's modern dolphins may have entered the water about 50 million years ago in the Eocene

Description

On average, bottlenose dolphins reach 10 to 14 feet in length and weigh about 1100 pounds. The dolphin's skin is dark gray on the back and light gray on the sides. To the naked eye, this fish can be distinguished from other dolphin species by its long roof.
Flukes (tails) and dorsal fins of dolphins are composed of connective tissue, without muscle or bone. The pectoral fins contain bone and muscle and are similar to that of a human arm. Bottlenose dolphins that live in colder, deeper waters tend to have more fat and blood than those that live in shallow water. The dolphin's elongated body helps it to swim very quickly - over 30 km / h.

Senses and intelligence

Dolphins have sharp eyesight, with two horseshoe-shaped pupils and a water cannon to aid vision in dim lighting conditions. The nose of the bottle has a poor sense of smell, as its blowhole opens only for breathing. Dolphins search for food by emitting clicking sounds and map their environment using echolocation. They have no vocal cords, but communicate through body language and whistling.
Bottlenose dolphins are extremely intelligent. Although dolphins cannot be found, they can understand artificial language, including sign language and human voices. They display mirror self-recognition, memory, understanding numbers, and using tools. They display high emotional intelligence, including altruistic behavior. Dolphins form complex social relationships.

Reproduction

Both male and female dolphins have genital slits that conceal their reproductive organs, making their bodies more hydrodynamic. Males compete with each other to mate with females during the breeding season. Spawning occurs at different times, depending on geographical location.
Pregnancy takes about 12 months. Usually, one young is born, although sometimes the mother carries twins. The calf stays with its mother and nurses from 18 months to 8 years. Males reach maturity between the ages of 5 and 13. Females mature between the ages of 9 and 14 and reproduce every 2 to 6 years. In the wild, bottlenose dolphins have a lifespan of 40 to 50 years. Females usually live 5 to 10 years longer than males. About 2% of dolphins live to be 60 years old. Bottlenose dolphins crossbreed with other dolphin species, both in captivity and in the wild.
