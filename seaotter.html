Sea otters - lovely animals

The sea otter (Enhydra lutris) is a recognizable and beloved marine mammal. They have furry bodies, bearded faces, and a tendency to lie on their backs and float on the water, a behavior humans see as evidence of enjoyment. They are native to the northern coasts of the Pacific Ocean, from northern Japan to Baja, Mexico. Most importantly, they are key species, which means their continued existence is necessary for some other species to survive.

describe

Sea otters are the smallest fully marine mammal in the world: Males are 3.9-4.9 feet long, while females are 3.3-4.6 feet long. The average body mass of males is about 88 pounds, with ranges ranging from 49-99 pounds; females between 31-73 pounds.
Balancing the temperature is a significant challenge for sea otters, which are not as blue as other marine mammals such as seals and walruses. Otters have thick coats made up of a combination of undercoat and longer guard hairs that provide insulation, but it must be maintained almost continuously. 10% of a sea otter's time is spent grooming its coat. However, feathers are an inflexible insulation, so when necessary, sea otters can cool off by patting their nearly hairless hind legs.

Habitat and distribution

Unlike some marine mammals such as whales, which die if left on land for too long, sea otters can come ashore to rest, groom or breastfeed. However, they spend most if not their entire lives in the water — Sea otters even give birth in the water.

Diet

Sea otters eat fish and marine invertebrates such as crabs, urchins, starfish and abalone, as well as squid and octopus. Some of these animals have hard shells to protect them from predators. But that's not a problem for the talented sea otters, which can crack their shells by smashing them with rocks.
To hunt, sea otters have been known to dive up to 320 feet deep; however, males mainly feed at depths of about 260 feet and females about 180 feet.
Sea otters have a large patch of skin under their forelimbs that is used for storage. They can keep extra food here and can also stock up on a favorite rock to shell their prey.

Reproduction and children

Sea otters reproduce sexually and that only happens when the females are in heat. Mating is polygenic - one male mates with all the females in his breeding territory. Gestation lasts for six months, and the female almost always gives birth to a single live cub, although mating does occur.
Young sea otters have extremely fleece-like fur that makes an otter so buoyant that it can't dive underwater and can float if not cared for properly. Before the mother otter leaves to feed her young, she wraps the otter in a piece of kelp to keep it in place. It takes 8-10 weeks for the puppy to shed its initial coat and learn to dive and the puppy stays with its mother for up to six months after birth. Females return to estrus within days to weeks after weaning.
